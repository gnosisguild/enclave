# Requestor Guide

This page is for **anyone using an Enclave-powered application** — voting in a private DAO, joining
a confidential auction, contributing data to a private analytics round, or anything else built on
top of Encrypted Execution Environments (E3s).

If you're a developer integrating with the Enclave contracts directly, see
[Building with Interfold](/building-with-enclave) and the [SDK reference](/sdk). The page you're
reading now is the plain-English version: what you're paying for, what to do when something goes
wrong, and how to read the security knobs on a request.

> Most of what an E3 does is invisible by design — your inputs are encrypted before they ever leave
> your device, and only the _result_ is revealed. This guide explains the few things that _are_
> visible to you: the fee, the waiting period, and what happens if a round fails.

## 1. What you're paying for

Every E3 request charges a one-time fee, paid in the protocol's fee token (USDC by default). The fee
covers four things, in roughly this order of cost:

1. **Selecting a Ciphernode committee** — a random subset of operators who will hold a share of the
   decryption key for your round.
2. **Generating the shared encryption key** (DKG). This is the cryptographically expensive step and
   is the largest line item on your bill.
3. **Running the actual computation** through the compute provider.
4. **Decrypting the result** and posting it on-chain.

A small protocol share (zero by default, configurable by governance, capped at 100%) is taken on
successful rounds and routed to the protocol treasury. Everything else is paid out to the operators
who actually did work.

The fee is **charged once, when you submit the request**, and held in escrow until the round
finishes. You don't get a refund on success — that's the price of the computation. You _do_ get a
refund (full or partial, depending on when it failed) on failure.

## 2. The lifecycle of a request

From the moment you submit until the result is published, every E3 walks through five phases:

| #   | Phase                 | What happens                                                                                   | What you see                              |
| --- | --------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- | ----------------------------------------- |
| 1   | **Request**           | Your fee is locked; a committee is chosen by random sortition.                                 | A "request submitted" confirmation.       |
| 2   | **Key generation**    | The committee runs a distributed key-generation ceremony and publishes a public key.           | Usually 5–20 minutes, depending on size.  |
| 3   | **Input window**      | You (and possibly other participants) encrypt your inputs to that key and submit them.         | Whatever input form the dApp shows.       |
| 4   | **Compute**           | The compute provider runs the program over the encrypted inputs and posts an encrypted result. | A waiting state — nothing for you to do.  |
| 5   | **Decrypt & publish** | The committee jointly decrypts the result and publishes it on-chain.                           | The plaintext result appears in the dApp. |

The application you're using will surface phase changes for you. If you want to inspect them
yourself, the Enclave contract emits an `E3StageChanged` event for every transition.

## 3. When things go wrong

A round can fail for a handful of reasons. Each one matches a specific phase, and each one comes
with a different refund split. The general rule is simple:

> **You get refunded for the work that wasn't completed.** Honest operators get paid for the work
> that _was_ completed. A small fixed share (default 5%) covers protocol overhead.

Concretely, every E3 that ends in a failed state is tagged with a `FailureReason`:

| Failure reason                 | When it happens                                                | Roughly what you get back                  |
| ------------------------------ | -------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------ |
| `CommitteeFormationTimeout`    | Not enough operators showed up to form a committee in time.    | ~95%                                       |
| `InsufficientCommitteeMembers` | The registry couldn't supply enough eligible operators.        | ~95%                                       |
| `DKGTimeout`                   | The committee never finished generating the encryption key.    | ~85%                                       |
| `DKGInvalidShares`             | An operator submitted bad key shares and was caught.           | ~85%                                       |
| `NoInputsReceived`             | Nobody submitted any inputs during the input window.           | ~55%                                       |
| `ComputeTimeout`               | The compute provider didn't post a result before its deadline. | ~55%                                       |
| `ComputeProviderExpired`       | The compute provider signalled it couldn't finish.             | ~55%                                       |
| `ComputeProviderFailed`        | The compute provider returned a verifiable error.              | ~55%                                       |
| `RequesterCancelled`           | You cancelled the round before the result was produced.        | ~55%                                       |
| `DecryptionTimeout`            | The committee never published the decryption.                  | ~0% (work was already done)                |
| `DecryptionInvalidShares`      | An operator submitted bad decryption shares and was caught.    | ~0% — but slashed bonds top up your refund |
| `VerificationFailed`           | The decryption proof didn't verify.                            | ~0%                                        |

> Percentages are based on the default refund policy. Governance can re-tune them; the live values
> are readable from `E3RefundManager.getWorkAllocation()` if you want to verify before requesting.

A useful mental model: the further along a round got before failing, the more of your fee was
already spent on legitimate work. Failures during decryption are the worst case for the requester's
refund, but they're also the case where misbehaving operators get **slashed** — and those slashed
bonds are added back to your refund automatically. In some failed rounds you can end up receiving
_more_ than your initial fee.

### How a failed round actually gets marked failed

Most failures are detected automatically by the protocol's slashing manager and mark the round
failed in the same transaction. A few timeout-based failures (the `*Timeout` reasons in the table)
need someone to _push_ the round across the finish line by calling `markE3Failed(e3Id)` on the
Enclave contract. **This call is permissionless** — the application you're using will usually do it
for you, but you can do it yourself from a block explorer if it's stalled.

### Claiming your refund

Refunds are pull-based: once a round is marked failed and the distribution is calculated, you have
to claim. The flow is:

1. Confirm the round is in `Failed` state. The application will usually show this; you can also
   check by reading `getE3Stage(e3Id)`.
2. Wait for the `RefundDistributionCalculated` event (it's emitted as soon as the slashing manager
   finalises the round — usually the same transaction that marked it failed).
3. Call `claimRequesterRefund(e3Id)` on the `E3RefundManager` contract from the **same address that
   originally submitted the request**. The refund is paid in the same token your original fee was
   paid in, even if governance has rotated the fee token in the meantime.

Most dApps will offer a "claim refund" button that does this for you. If you submitted from a
smart-contract wallet (e.g. Safe), use the same wallet to claim.

There's no deadline for claiming. Your refund sits in the refund manager indefinitely until you take
it.

## 4. Things to know before you submit

A few practical points that catch people out:

- **Approve only the exact fee.** Use the dApp's "estimate" / `getE3Quote` flow to read the exact
  fee, and approve that amount. Don't grant unlimited approvals to the Enclave contract.
- **You can't top up later.** If the protocol's pricing changes after you submit, your locked fee is
  what runs your round. Don't try to amend an in-flight request — submit a new one if needed.
- **You can cancel before compute starts.** Cancellation routes through the protocol's standard
  failure path (`RequesterCancelled`) and gives you the matching partial refund. You will still pay
  for sortition and key generation that already ran.
- **Set realistic windows.** If you're configuring the round yourself rather than using a preset,
  give the input window enough time for participants to actually submit, and the compute window
  enough time for your worst observed program runtime.
- **Refunds don't expire**, but they also don't auto-claim. Set a reminder to come back, or use a
  dApp that claims for you.

## 5. Choosing a secure committee

If you're configuring an E3 yourself (most users won't be — applications usually pick sensible
defaults), the two parameters that matter are the **committee size `n`** and the **decryption
threshold `m`**.

The high-level intuition:

- `n` is **how many operators** are randomly selected to safeguard your round.
- `m` is **how many of those `n` operators** must cooperate to decrypt the result.

### Picking `n` (committee size)

`n` controls both **availability** and **collusion resistance**. A larger committee survives more
simultaneous outages and is harder to bribe, but every additional operator adds coordination work to
every other operator, so fees grow roughly quadratically in `n`. The right choice is a function of
how much value is at stake in the round and how deep the live operator set is at the time.

General guidelines:

- Keep `n` comfortably below the number of currently registered operators, so sortition has
  meaningful entropy.
- Use a small `n` for development and rehearsal rounds where cost matters more than security.
- Use a larger `n` for production rounds where the value being protected justifies the extra fee and
  longer key-generation time.
- Going beyond a few dozen operators rarely buys meaningful additional security and primarily
  inflates the fee.

### Picking `m` (decryption threshold)

`m` is the dial between **safety** (no-one can decrypt your data without `m` cooperating operators)
and **liveness** (the round can only finish if at least `m` are online).

General guidelines:

- A bare majority is the absolute floor: anything less than `m ≥ ⌈n / 2⌉ + 1` means a minority could
  collude to decrypt your round on its own.
- A two-thirds threshold (`m ≈ ⌈2n / 3⌉`) tracks the standard byzantine-fault-tolerant trust
  assumption and is a sensible middle ground.
- Tighter thresholds (closer to `n`) increase safety against collusion but make the round more
  fragile — if too few operators are online when decryption time arrives, the round will time out.
- Never set `m == n`. A single offline operator will then end the round with no decryption.

To make the random committee draw less variable, **increase `n`** rather than push the `m / n` ratio
higher — doubling `n` roughly halves the chance the random sample is unusually unlucky.

## 6. At-a-glance checklist

Before you click "submit":

- [ ] You've read the dApp's fee estimate and approved exactly that amount.
- [ ] The committee size and threshold are appropriate for the value of the round.
- [ ] The input window leaves enough time for everyone (including you) to submit.
- [ ] You have a way to receive a notification when the round completes or fails.
- [ ] You know which address to claim a refund from if the round fails.

After the round ends:

- [ ] If it succeeded, you can read the result on-chain or in the dApp.
- [ ] If it failed, wait for the `RefundDistributionCalculated` event, then claim from the Refund
      Manager using the same address that submitted the request.
